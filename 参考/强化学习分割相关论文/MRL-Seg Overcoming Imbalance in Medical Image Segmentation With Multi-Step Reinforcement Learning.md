# Summary of MRL-Seg: Overcoming Imbalance in Medical Image Segmentation With Multi-Step Reinforcement Learning
## (1) Problem to Be Solved
This paper aims to address the critical challenge of **severe pixel-level class imbalance in medical image segmentation**, which has two core manifestations and leads to critical limitations of existing methods:
1.  **Inter-class imbalance**: In binary segmentation tasks, the number of foreground pixels (regions of interest, ROI, e.g., tumors/lesions) is drastically smaller than background pixels, often spanning several orders of magnitude across different slices of the same patient.
2.  **Intra-foreground discrepancy**: There is extreme size variation among foreground targets, with lesions ranging from just a few dozen pixels to tens of thousands of pixels in different CT slices.

Existing methods (including U-Net/Transformer-based segmentation models, re-weighting/re-sampling strategies, modified loss functions, and two-stage reinforcement learning (RL) localization-then-segmentation pipelines) have critical limitations:
- Cross-entropy (CE) loss, the most widely used training objective, assumes equal contribution of all pixels, leading to model bias toward majority background classes and failure to capture minority foreground lesions.
- CNN-based models lack the ability to model long-range dependencies, while Transformer-based models suffer from insufficient inductive bias for medical images, both resulting in positioning offset, unstable edge segmentation, and high false positive rates in imbalanced scenarios.
- Existing RL-based segmentation methods rely heavily on the accuracy of initial lesion localization, cannot adapt to highly variable lesion sizes, and fail to simultaneously achieve robust segmentation of tiny and large targets.
- All above methods lead to degraded generalization performance in highly imbalanced medical image segmentation, which hinders reliable clinical diagnosis and research.

## (2) Proposed Method
### Workflow of MRL-Seg
The proposed MRL-Seg (Multi-step Reinforcement Learning for Segmentation) is an end-to-end closed-loop framework consisting of three core modules: **Reinforcement-Net**, **Representation-Net**, and **Segmentation-Net**. The overall workflow is as follows:
1.  **Pre-training Initialization**: The Segmentation-Net (a hybrid U-shaped architecture combining CNN and Transformer) is pre-trained on the target dataset first. Its weights are used to initialize the feature extraction blocks of the Reinforcement-Net, ensuring training stability and convergence of the RL module.
2.  **State Representation**: The Representation-Net takes the input medical image and provides the pixel-level state \(s^{(t)}\) for the Reinforcement-Net at each step \(t\).
3.  **RL Decision-Making**: The Reinforcement-Net, built on the Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) framework, treats each pixel (represented by a patch centered on it) as an independent agent. It samples an action from the action space \(\{\text{mask}, \text{retain}\}\) for each agent, where `mask` removes pixels adverse to segmentation and `retain` preserves pixels beneficial to the task.
4.  **Segmentation and Reward Calculation**: After all actions are sampled, the Representation-Net generates the refined image representation and feeds it into the Segmentation-Net. The Segmentation-Net outputs the pixel-level segmentation result, which is used to calculate the environmental reward and feed it back to the Reinforcement-Net.
5.  **Closed-Loop Iterative Optimization**: The Reinforcement-Net updates its policy and value functions based on the received reward. Through continuous multi-step iteration, the framework gradually masks non-ROI background pixels, balances the foreground-background distribution, and optimizes segmentation performance slice by slice.

### Key Innovations (Focus on Reinforcement Learning)
The core innovations of MRL-Seg lie in its novel RL-based design for imbalanced medical image segmentation, with the following key breakthroughs:
1.  **Pixel-level Multi-agent Multi-step RL Paradigm for Segmentation**
    The framework models patch-based medical image representation learning as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), and solves it via multi-step reinforcement learning. To address the intractable large exploration space in pixel-level RL tasks, each pixel is treated as an independent agent with a shared pixel-level behavior policy. Fully Convolutional Networks (FCNs) are adopted to enable parameter sharing across all N agents, which allows the model to handle input images of varying sizes, and transforms the pixel-level segmentation task into a sequential decision-making problem without modifying complex loss functions or adaptive segmentation windows.

2.  **A3C-based Reinforcement-Net Architecture Design**
    The Reinforcement-Net is built on the A3C framework, with a shared feature extraction backbone (3 convolution blocks + 1 Transformer block) and two separate heads: a policy head (Actor-Net) that predicts the optimal action policy \(\pi(a^{(t)}|s^{(t)})\) for each patch, and a value head (Critic-Net) that evaluates the expected total reward \(V(s^{(t)})\) of the current state. This design enables asynchronous policy and value updates, and effectively mitigates the severe imbalance between ROI and non-ROI regions by intelligently masking non-informative background pixels.

3.  **Medical Prior Knowledge-Infused Reward Function Design (Core RL Innovation)**
    Two types of medical image prior knowledge are integrated into the reward function design, providing explicit and clinically meaningful guidance for RL policy optimization without additional manual annotation:
    - **Foreground-Background (FB) Ratio Prior for Global Reward**: A balance constraint term is added to the global reward function, which constrains the foreground-background ratio to the range of 44.7% to 54.8% via hyperparameter tuning. This term guides the agent to mask non-ROI patches, gradually transforming the severely imbalanced class distribution into a balanced one, and reducing distractors for segmentation.
    - **Approximate Difficulty of the Pixels (ADP) Prior for Local Reward**: A novel ADP metric is proposed to quantify the segmentation difficulty of each pixel, calculated as the ratio of pixels with different labels in the local receptive field of the target pixel. The ADP metric is integrated into the multi-step local reward function, with a convolution-weighted n-step return design that incorporates the value of neighboring pixels. This design enhances the model's focus on hard-to-segment boundary pixels, significantly improving the localization accuracy of lesion outlines and the stability of edge segmentation.

4.  **Stable RL Training via Closed-Loop Synergy with Segmentation Module**
    The pre-trained Segmentation-Net provides a stable and reliable reward signal for RL training, and its pre-trained weights warm-start the Reinforcement-Net, solving the critical challenge of difficulty in convergence when training RL models from scratch. In turn, the RL-optimized image representation from the Representation-Net continuously improves the segmentation performance of the Segmentation-Net, forming a mutually reinforcing closed-loop system.

### Input, Output and Label Requirements
1.  **Input**: The input of MRL-Seg is the original 2D medical image slices (including CT and ultrasound images). All input images are resized to a standardized 512×512 resolution, with CT Hounsfield unit values clipped to [-79, 304] and pixel values rescaled to [0, 255]. No additional pre-processing or manual annotation of ROI regions is required.
2.  **Output**: The main output is a pixel-level binary segmentation mask with the same spatial size as the input image, which distinguishes the foreground ROI (lesion/target organ) from the background. The framework also outputs two auxiliary outputs: the action decision (mask/retain) for each pixel patch from the Reinforcement-Net, and the refined image representation from the Representation-Net.
3.  **Label Requirements**: During both training and testing phases, the framework **only requires the pixel-level ground truth segmentation mask**, and does not need any additional labels or annotations. Specifically, no organ labels, lesion bounding boxes, lesion size labels, segmentation difficulty annotations, or other manual prior labels are used in any stage of the model training and inference. All medical prior knowledge is embedded into the design of the reward function, eliminating the need for extra annotation costs.
