# Summary of SPARS: Self-Play Adversarial Reinforcement Learning for Segmentation of Liver Tumours
## (1) Problem to Be Solved
1.  **Clinical and technical bottlenecks of manual segmentation**: Accurate liver tumour segmentation is essential for cancer diagnosis, biopsy planning, radiotherapy delivery, and other targeted interventions. However, manual voxel-level delineation of tumours on 3D CT scans is extremely labor-intensive, highly subjective, and suffers from significant inter- and intra-observer variability, which impairs the reproducibility and reliability of tumour localization in clinical practice.
2.  **Limitations of fully-supervised learning**: State-of-the-art fully-supervised segmentation models rely on large-scale, high-cost voxel-level expert annotations for training. These dense labels are difficult and expensive to curate, and their inherent subjectivity and high variance severely degrade the generalizability of trained models, even with large datasets.
3.  **Performance gaps in existing weakly-supervised methods**: While weakly-supervised semantic segmentation (WSSS) using low-cost image-level labels has been explored to mitigate annotation burdens, existing WSSS methods fail to achieve performance comparable to fully-supervised models for complex clinical liver tumour localization tasks. Additionally, most existing methods have fixed localization termination conditions and lack application-specific flexibility for clinical deployment.
4.  **Gap between objective labels and segmentation tasks**: Histopathology-derived image-level binary cancer presence labels are more objective and easier to obtain than dense annotations, but there is a lack of an effective framework to bridge these weak image-level labels to the voxel-level tumour segmentation objective for in vivo clinical CT scans.

## (2) Proposed Method
### Overall Pipeline
The proposed SPARS (Self-Play Adversarial Reinforcement Learning for Segmentation) is a novel weakly-supervised semantic segmentation framework, with a 4-stage workflow as follows:
1.  **Object Presence Classifier Training**: A 3D convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier is trained on image-level binary cancer presence/absence labels, using binary cross-entropy loss to optimize network parameters. The trained classifier outputs a continuous probability score of tumour presence for any given input image window, acting as the core supervision signal for the subsequent reinforcement learning (RL) module.
2.  **Markov Decision Process (MDP) Environment Formulation**: The tumour localization task is formalized as an MDP for the RL module:
    - **State**: Fixed-size 3D cropped windows from the input CT scan, representing the local region observed by the agent at each time step.
    - **Action**: Discrete 3D movement of the window in 6 orthogonal directions (±height, ±width, ±depth) with a fixed step size, with only one direction of movement allowed per time step.
    - **State Transition**: A deterministic update of the window position based on the action selected by the agent, generating the next observed state.
    - **Policy**: Two stochastic policies (agents) with identical network architectures and initialized parameters, which map the current observed state to the optimal window movement action.
3.  **Self-Play Adversarial Policy Optimization**: Two competing agents interact with the CT image environment simultaneously. The reward for each agent is determined by a pairwise comparison of the tumour presence probability scores (from the pre-trained classifier) of their respective current windows: an agent receives a +1 reward if its window’s score is higher than its opponent’s, and a -1 reward otherwise, with the opponent’s reward set as the inverse. The policy is optimized to maximize the expected cumulative discounted reward over the trajectory, using proximal policy optimization (PPO) in practice.
4.  **Inference and Voxel-Level Segmentation Generation**: For an unseen 3D CT scan, the optimized policy initializes a window at the center of the image and generates a trajectory of window movements until the classifier score of the current window exceeds a pre-defined termination threshold. A continuous voxel-level probability map is constructed by accumulating the classifier scores of all windows along the trajectory for each corresponding voxel. The final binary liver tumour segmentation mask is obtained by thresholding this probability map, with the threshold adjustable for application-specific clinical needs.

### Core Innovations (Focus on Reinforcement Learning Design)
1.  **Dual-agent self-play adversarial RL framework for WSSS**: Unlike single-agent RL-based WSSS methods, the competing dual-agent design minimizes the negative impact of classifier prediction bias caused by the domain gap between full CT images and local windows. Since rewards are based on relative score comparisons rather than absolute classifier outputs, the framework is robust to classifier prediction inaccuracies. This design also accelerates policy convergence by leveraging experience from dual-agent interactions.
2.  **Classifier-guided reward mechanism for end-to-end weak supervision**: The pre-trained object presence classifier (trained exclusively on image-level labels) serves as the sole supervision signal for the RL module, eliminating the need for any dense annotations during RL training. This design effectively bridges the semantic gap between weak image-level classification labels and the dense voxel-level segmentation objective.
3.  **Continuous voxel-level probability map generation with high flexibility**: Unlike previous patch-based RL WSSS methods that only output binary patch-level inclusion/exclusion decisions, SPARS accumulates classifier scores across the agent’s full movement trajectory to generate a continuous voxel-level probability map. This allows adjustable thresholding for the final segmentation, providing far greater application-specific flexibility compared to methods with fixed localization termination conditions.
4.  **Stabilized adversarial training for robust tumour localization**: The stochastic policy design with identical parameter initialization for the two agents ensures stable and balanced adversarial competition, avoiding mode collapse and enabling robust localization of liver tumours even when trained with an extremely small number of weak image-level labels.

### Input and Output
- **Input**:
  - For training: 3D portal venous phase abdominal CT scans, paired with image-level binary labels indicating the presence or absence of clinically significant liver tumours.
  - For inference: Unlabeled raw 3D abdominal CT scans of the liver.
- **Output**: A voxel-level binary segmentation mask of liver tumours for the input CT scan, alongside a continuous probability map that quantifies the likelihood of tumour presence for each individual voxel.

### Required Labels
The SPARS framework **does not require voxel-level segmentation mask labels** for model training. The only label required for the entire training process is **image-level binary labels (cancer present/absent)** for each CT scan, which can be feasibly and objectively sourced from clinical biopsy and histopathology reports. No additional manual annotations (such as bounding boxes, scribbles, or pixel-level delineations) are needed at any stage of model training or inference.
